The Problem

Problem Description

Prior projects have scraped around the surface of why Cal Poly’s waste situation is as it is today. You only see what’s visible to the student body at large: trash bins, recycling, compost bins in certain locations, custodial staff, green groups and clubs, etc. What you don’t see is the behind-the-scenes relations that make up the core of Cal Poly’s waste stream and how it relates to post consumer waste.

Institutional Context

The need for this project arose from an observed lack of communication between the the stakeholder’s in Cal Poly’s waste stream. There are so many moving parts, so many projects that have tried to create positive change, and most importantly, there is so much at stake for the future of our sustainability. Join us in our quest to make institutional change and demystify the waste stream.

Project Description

Our aim is to foster a better understanding about the strengths and weaknesses of the Cal Poly waste stream at an institutional level. This includes students, faculty, facilities, and all interested parties and stakeholders. Ultimately, this will allow for better communication between stakeholders and will facilitate more sustainable policies on campus regarding consumer waste.

Three Phase Project Timeline

  • Present

    PolyWaste Website

    We have created a web resource to educate all interested constituents about the specific flows of Cal Poly’s waste stream. The central component of this site is a GIS map that allows viewers to easily understand and interact with the waste stream data.

  • Phase I

    Update GIS Data

    The last waste audit and bin location analysis was conducted in 2015. As a result, the data on the current GIS system is outdated and provides few insights. This first phase involves streamlining the means of uploading data to the GIS system and identifying locations of new waste bins.

  • Phase II

    Overhaul Waste Recording System on Campus

    Facilities currently has no system in place to measure quantitative and qualitative waste data. Facilities managers should be given iPads so they can easily upload quantitative (waste bin volume, expressed as a percentage) and qualitative (contamination) data into the GIS system. This will enable trends in the waste stream to be readily apparent over time.

  • Phase III

    Conduct Campus Wide Waste Audit (Fall 2019)

    The final phase includes a waste audit that will be conducted during the next academic year (2019). The data from this audit will be uploaded to the existing GIS portal so that key players can compare the strengths and weaknesses of Cal Poly’s waste stream between all on-campus buildings. The audit can be implemented as an annual event, as the locations of bins, waste content, and waste collection points will change continuously.

Future Project Inspirations

Bin Identification System

Currently, there is no efficient system in place to identify waste receptacles on the Cal Poly campus. A critical first step is to develop a waste bin identification system. Without an identification system, it will be difficult to effectively manage post-consumer waste. Although many organizations use RFID tags to optimize efficiency and traceability, such a high-tech system is not necessary for Cal Poly. Future students could create waste bin labels and a corresponding database. This database should include location data, collection data, and receptacle type.

Update Bin Locations

Once a waste bin identification system is in place, another team of ambitious students could take on the task of updating the location of waste bins in the GIS map. Since the initial waste bin location audit in 2015, new waste bins have been added and locations of previous bins have been changed. One component of this project would involve physically noting the updated location of each bin and linking this information to the database. Another component would involve uploading the updated waste receptacle locations to the GIS map.

Build Waste Audit Manual

A comprehensive waste audit will be conducted during the next academic year (2019). In preparation, a future EDES408 team or other group of passionate Cal Poly students could build a waste audit manual. This would include recommendations for weight vs. volume based assessments and methodologies, supplies, labor, and health and safety precautions, and follow-up audit recommendations for efficient waste management on the Cal Poly campus.

Battle of the Waste

Green Campus Waste Challenge!

Implementing Post-Consumer Composting on Cal Poly

Campus to Compost details the history of composting on campus, it's unfortunate closure, the current conditions of our limited compost infrastructure and operations, and how to bring back composting to its former glory. Plastic Bag Ban

Plastic Bag Public Service Announcement

Inspired by San Francisco’s county wide ban of plastic bags in 2007, this team sought to retell the story of plastic bags through the light of a PSA. Inciting an emotional appeal by creating startling visuals regarding the devastating effects of plastic bags, team “Rebag” crafted a useful framework for instigating change on a local scale. The Rebag team understood that meta-environmental change must begin at the cultural level. We recommend that a similar strategy be used when tackling the post-consumer waste stream issue at Cal Poly.

Problem Description




SCRAPI was designed in order to address the waste stream being generated from the College of Architecture and Environmental Design, in the form of model material scrap storage over waste bins within the department. The aim was to raise awareness about the footprint of model-making within a school that supposedly cares about sustainability.

For instance, you may have seen or heard prior projects proposed or conducted by classes such as EDES 408, or even from student run organizations such as the Zero Waste club, and from facility groups such as the Energy, Utilities, and Sustainability organization. That being said and done, those are small actions that have yet to trigger change on campus. Why?

The obvious problem, is money. Money is a primary driver of projects to take off, as you can only do so much with limited funding. It may be assumed that Cal Poly does not allocate sufficient levels of funding to address the issues surrounding waste ranging from logistics to implementation. After all, the decision-makers on campus have their own priorities and leadership scope, and some may not see eye to eye with those lower in the campus hierarchy. So politics and leadership are segway problems to waste.

Then we look at the environment at Cal Poly. We are no different from other colleges in that we sell to our students waste. Our consumerism loop is not sustainable in that we are not offered sustainable goods and services, and all we do once we consume those consumer goods is it ends up in a landfill like Cold Canyon. Some groups in the past have tried to address sustainable products and goods, but that’s a drop in the consumerism ocean here at Poly.

Infrastructure is a glaring problem as well. Cal Poly’s waste is collected and transported to the Cold Canyon Landfill for waste and a facility that handles compostables down in Santa Maria. SLO does not have infrastructure in place to help facilitate sustainability of the city and the campus, and thus our waste stream, even if the interior is reworked, means nothing if the external systems cannot follow up.

Student habits are another aspect of the waste environment at Poly. We are raised from different cultures, backgrounds, and we carry on our consumerism and waste habits we learned from our parents. Not everyone will have the same sustainability mindset when it comes to waste and consuming.

In the grand scope of this problem, we as Group 8 of EDES 408 Spring 18, have started this project from questioning the other wastes besides food that is handled via Campus Dining. But as the research and questions started to dive deeper, and past projects started to surface, it was then clear that although people understand the issues related to waste, there was no clear nexus between the players involved. Similar ideas, visions, and challenges were repeated as common themes in past and present projects, and some ideas, though innovative as they sound, are not accessible to the greater Cal Poly audience of students, faculty, facility, decision-makers, and visitors. Lack of effective communication and nexus seemed to be the core problem in trying to address Cal Poly’s post consumerism waste stream. Note that communication and nexus is not the end-means of solving this problem. It is meant to instill and inspire dialogue, ideas, collaboration, and get people to see connections and ideas that have existed before their time, so that effective action and progress can truly be achieved going forward.
